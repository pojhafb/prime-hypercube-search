\begin{abstract}
We study prime numbers as vertices in the $m$-bit binary hypercube
$Q_m = \{0,1\}^m$, where adjacency is defined by Hamming distance rather than
arithmetic distance. This yields a search geometry fundamentally different from
the classical number line. We ask: given an integer $x$, how close is it to a
prime in Hamming distance, and can Hamming-space search find primes efficiently?

We make four contributions. \emph{First}, we show experimentally that nearly
every $m$-bit integer is within Hamming distance 2 of a prime for $m \leq 20$,
a consequence of the rapid growth of Hamming balls relative to the decay of prime
density. \emph{Second}, we identify a directional bias in the hypercube: bit
positions controlling low-order modular residues are more useful for reaching
primes, with bit~0 (parity) overwhelmingly dominant in the full hypercube.
\emph{Third}, restricting search to the \emph{odd subcube}---fixing bit~0 to
remove the parity advantage---we show that standard odd-increment search achieves
fewer average candidate checks than any Hamming strategy, but odd-subcube Hamming
search wins or ties standard search on approximately 58--60\% of individual
starting points across $m \in \{24, 28, 32\}$.
\emph{Fourth}, a Pareto analysis reveals that the two strategies are not
competitors but expose distinct geometries: standard search minimizes arithmetic
distance to the found prime; Hamming search minimizes bit-level perturbation.
These are genuinely different objectives, and neither dominates on both axes.

We release all code and data at
\url{https://github.com/pojhafb/prime-hypercube-search}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ---------------------------------------------------------------

Prime numbers are classically studied as points on the integer number line, where
distance is measured by subtraction: $|p - x|$.  The prime number theorem
\citep{hadamard1896, delavalleepoussin1896} tells us that primes are sparse:
among integers near $N$, about $1/\ln N$ of them are prime. The expected gap to
the next prime from $x$ is approximately $\ln x$.

Every integer also has a binary representation, and binary strings of length $m$
are naturally placed as vertices of the $m$-dimensional hypercube $Q_m = \{0,1\}^m$.
Two vertices are adjacent if they differ in exactly one bit. The number of
differing bit positions between two integers $x$ and $y$ is the \emph{Hamming
distance}:
\[
  \dH(x, y) = |\{i : \text{bit}_i(x) \neq \text{bit}_i(y)\}|.
\]
This defines a metric on $Q_m$ with no direct relationship to arithmetic distance.

This paper asks: \emph{what does the distribution of primes look like in Hamming
geometry?} More concretely, can one find a prime near a given integer $x$ by
searching within a small Hamming ball around $x$, and how does such search compare
to the standard algorithm of testing $x, x+2, x+4, \ldots$?

\subsection{Why this question is interesting}

There are two independent motivations.

\paragraph{Geometric.}
The primes are a fixed, arithmetically-defined set. Placing them in two different
metric spaces---the integers $\Z$ with the usual absolute value, and the hypercube
$Q_m$ with Hamming distance---and comparing their local structure is a natural
question in combinatorial number theory. It has connections to coding theory
\citep{macwilliams1977} and to the study of prime gaps \citep{granville1995}.

\paragraph{Computational.}
Finding a prime near a given large integer is a basic primitive in cryptographic
key generation \citep{menezes1996}. Standard algorithms test $x, x+2, x+4, \ldots$
and rely on the prime number theorem for their expected cost. An alternative
search in Hamming space might find primes with different characteristics---in
particular, primes that are arithmetically far from $x$ but differ in only a few
bits. Such bit-close primes have potential relevance in applications where
bit-level structure matters (e.g., hardware-friendly prime selection).

\subsection{Summary of results}

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item \textbf{Hamming density.} For $m = 14$ (full scan) and $m \in \{16, 18, 20\}$
    (sampled), nearly every integer is within Hamming distance 2 of a prime.
    Specifically, in the full $m=14$ scan, 98.4\% of integers are within distance 2,
    and 100\% are within distance 3.

  \item \textbf{Directional bias.} In the full hypercube, bit~0 (the parity bit)
    dominates the flip-position distribution because it determines oddness.
    After restricting to the odd subcube (bit~0 fixed), low-order bits retain a
    modest advantage attributable to small modular constraints.

  \item \textbf{Parity correction matters.} A learned bit-order policy trained on
    one dimension and transferred to a larger dimension captures mostly the parity
    signal. After the odd subcube correction, learned policies provide only a small
    improvement over simple low-bit-first ordering.

  \item \textbf{Competitive but not superior.} In the odd subcube, standard
    odd-increment search achieves lower average candidate checks for all tested
    dimensions. However, low-bit-first Hamming search wins or ties on 58--60\% of
    individual starting points.

  \item \textbf{Two geometries.} The Pareto frontier for (avg checks, avg Hamming
    distance) includes both standard and Hamming strategies, confirming they are
    not substitutes. Standard search finds arithmetically close primes.
    Hamming search finds bit-close primes. These are different problems.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Mathematical Model}
\label{sec:model}
%% ---------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{The binary hypercube}

\begin{definition}
  The \emph{$m$-bit binary hypercube} is the graph $Q_m = (\{0,1\}^m, E)$
  where $\{x, y\} \in E$ if and only if $\dH(x, y) = 1$.
\end{definition}

We identify each vertex with the integer it encodes in binary, so $Q_m$ contains
the integers $\{0, 1, \ldots, 2^m - 1\}$.

\begin{definition}
  The \emph{Hamming ball} of radius $r$ centered at $x$ is:
  \[
    \mathcal{B}(x, r) = \{y \in Q_m : \dH(x, y) \leq r\}.
  \]
  Its size depends only on $m$ and $r$:
  \[
    |\mathcal{B}(x, r)| = B(m, r) = \sum_{i=0}^{r} \binom{m}{i}.
  \]
\end{definition}

\subsection{Primes in the hypercube}

\begin{definition}
  The \emph{prime set} of $Q_m$ is $P_m = \{x \in Q_m : x \text{ is prime}\}$.
  The \emph{Hamming distance to the nearest prime} from $x$ is:
  \[
    D_m(x) = \min_{p \in P_m} \dH(x, p).
  \]
\end{definition}

By the prime number theorem, $|P_m| \approx 2^m / (m \ln 2)$, so the prime
density in $Q_m$ is approximately $\delta_m = 1/(m \ln 2)$.

\subsection{The odd subcube}

All primes greater than 2 are odd. This means $P_m \subset Q_m^{\text{odd}}$
for $m \geq 2$, where:
\[
  Q_m^{\text{odd}} = \{x \in Q_m : \text{bit}_0(x) = 1\}.
\]
$Q_m^{\text{odd}}$ is the odd subcube, a sub-hypercube of dimension $m-1$
with $2^{m-1}$ vertices. All Hamming search in this paper operates within
$Q_m^{\text{odd}}$ by fixing bit~0 to 1 and flipping only bits $1, \ldots, m-1$.

\section{Search Strategies}
\label{sec:strategies}
%% ---------------------------------------------------------------

Given a starting integer $x$, we define a prime search as producing a result
$(p, k, \dH(x_0, p), |p - x_0|)$ where $x_0 = x \,|\, 1$ (force odd),
$p$ is the found prime, and $k$ is the number of primality tests performed.

We compare six strategies.

\subsection{Standard odd-increment search (SOI)}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Standard odd-increment search}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State $x_0 \gets x \,|\, 1$
\State $k \gets 0$, $c \gets x_0$
\While{$c$ is not prime}
  \State $k \gets k + 1$, $c \gets c + 2$
\EndWhile
\Return $(c,\, k,\, \dH(x_0, c),\, c - x_0)$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

This is the standard algorithm used in practice. It always finds the smallest
prime $\geq x_0$ and minimizes arithmetic distance.

\subsection{Odd-subcube Hamming search}

Let $\mathcal{F}(m, r_{\max})$ be a list of flip sets --- tuples of bit positions
in $\{1, \ldots, m-1\}$ --- ordered by some policy $\pi$.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Odd-subcube Hamming search}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State $x_0 \gets x \,|\, 1$
\State $k \gets 0$
\For{each flip set $S \in \mathcal{F}(m, r_{\max})$ ordered by $\pi$}
  \State $k \gets k + 1$
  \State $c \gets x_0 \oplus \bigoplus_{b \in S} 2^b$
  \If{$c$ is prime}
    \Return $(c,\, k,\, |S|,\, |c - x_0|)$
  \EndIf
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The choice of ordering $\pi$ gives the following concrete strategies:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{LBF} (\emph{low-bit-first}): sort flip sets by $(\sum S, \max S)$,
    preferring lower bit indices.
  \item \textbf{HBF} (\emph{high-bit-first}): sort by $(-\sum S, -\max S)$.
  \item \textbf{RND} (\emph{uniform random}): shuffle flip sets randomly.
  \item \textbf{LRN} (\emph{learned bit-order}): sort by learned bit scores
    (Section~\ref{sec:learned}).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Hybrid strategy}

The hybrid strategy interleaves SOI candidates and learned Hamming candidates,
deduplicating before testing:

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Hybrid standard + learned search}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State $x_0 \gets x \,|\, 1$, $\text{seen} \gets \emptyset$, $k \gets 0$
\For{$i = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$}
  \If{$i < L$} add $x_0 + 2i$ to candidates \EndIf
  \If{$i < |\mathcal{F}|$} add $x_0 \oplus \text{mask}(\mathcal{F}_i)$ to candidates \EndIf
  \For{each candidate $c$ not in $\text{seen}$}
    \State $\text{seen} \gets \text{seen} \cup \{c\}$, $k \gets k+1$
    \If{$c$ is prime} \Return $(c, k, \dH(x_0,c), |c - x_0|)$ \EndIf
  \EndFor
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

$L$ is the odd-increment limit (default 128).

\subsection{Learned bit-order policy}
\label{sec:learned}

We learn per-bit usefulness scores from data. Given training dimension $m_{\text{tr}}$
and $N_{\text{tr}}$ random odd starting points:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Run LBF on each starting point and record $S^*$ (the successful flip set).
  \item For each bit $b \in S^*$, increment $\text{score}[b]$.
  \item Normalize: bits with zero count get score $0.25 \times \min_{\text{score}>0}$.
  \item Bit~0 is forced to score 0 (excluded from odd subcube).
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Transfer.}
For test dimension $m_{\text{te}} > m_{\text{tr}}$:
bits $\{1,\ldots,m_{\text{tr}}-1\}$ keep learned scores;
new bits $\{m_{\text{tr}},\ldots,m_{\text{te}}-1\}$ receive the fallback score
$0.25 \times \min_{\text{score}>0}$.

\section{Theoretical Analysis}
\label{sec:theory}
%% ---------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Expected checks for SOI}

By Bertrand's postulate \citep{bertrand1845}, there is always a prime between
$n$ and $2n$. The prime number theorem gives a sharper bound: the expected gap
between consecutive primes near $N$ is $\ln N$. Among odd candidates the gap
halves, so:
\[
  \E[\text{checks for SOI}] \approx \frac{\ln(2^m)}{2} = \frac{m \ln 2}{2}.
\]

For $m = 32$: $\approx 11.1$. For $m = 64$: $\approx 22.2$.

\subsection{Hamming ball size and prime density}

The expected number of primes within Hamming radius $r$ of a random odd
$m$-bit integer is approximately:
\[
  \E[|P_m \cap \mathcal{B}(x_0, r)|]
    \approx B(m-1, r) \cdot \frac{2}{m \ln 2}
\]
where the factor of 2 accounts for restricting to the odd subcube.

For $m = 32$, $r = 4$:
\[
  B(31, 4) = 1 + 31 + 465 + 4495 + 31465 = 36457,
  \quad
  \frac{2}{32 \ln 2} \approx 0.090,
\]
\[
  \E[\text{primes in radius-4 ball}] \approx 3290.
\]

So the radius-4 ball contains thousands of primes in expectation. The question
for Hamming search is not \emph{whether} a prime exists nearby, but \emph{how
many checks} are needed with a fixed ordering to reach one.

\subsection{Why SOI dominates average checks}

SOI tests candidates in the direction of increasing arithmetic value, and the
prime number theorem guarantees the expected gap. Hamming search visits
candidates in bit-flip order, which does not align with the arithmetic
distribution of primes. Specifically:

\begin{observation}
A Hamming flip of bit $k$ changes $x$ by $\pm 2^k$, which can be a very large
arithmetic step. Many candidates in the Hamming ball are arithmetically far from
$x$ and from each other, so Hamming search does not benefit from the local
density of primes near $x$ on the number line.
\end{observation}

This explains why SOI achieves lower average checks: it leverages arithmetic
prime density, which Hamming search ignores.

\subsection{Why Hamming search wins on many individual inputs}

\begin{observation}
For any starting point $x_0$, if the nearest prime in Hamming distance is at
distance $d$ and position $k$ in the LBF ordering, Hamming search finds it in
$k$ checks. SOI might need $\Omega(2^{d_{\min}})$ arithmetic steps to reach
the same prime.
\end{observation}

When a prime happens to be close in Hamming distance and early in the ordering,
Hamming search wins decisively. This accounts for the $\sim$37--39\% of inputs
where LBF beats SOI.

\section{Pareto Analysis}
\label{sec:pareto}
%% ---------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Pareto dominance}

\begin{definition}
Strategy $A$ \emph{Pareto-dominates} strategy $B$ on metrics $(f_1, f_2)$
(both to be minimized) if $f_1(A) \leq f_1(B)$ and $f_2(A) \leq f_2(B)$
with at least one strict inequality. The \emph{Pareto frontier} is the set of
non-dominated strategies.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Checks vs. Hamming distance}

On the (avg checks, avg $\dH$) frontier at $m=32$:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{SOI}: lowest checks (11.1), but highest Hamming distance (8.4).
  \item \textbf{LBF}: lowest Hamming distance (1.8), moderate checks (19.4).
  \item \textbf{LRN}: close to LBF on both axes.
  \item \textbf{Hybrid}: intermediate on both axes.
\end{itemize}

Both SOI and LBF are on the Pareto frontier. Neither dominates the other.

\subsection{Checks vs. arithmetic distance}

On the (avg checks, avg $\log_2(1 + |p - x_0|)$) frontier:

SOI dominates nearly all Hamming strategies: it achieves both fewer checks
\emph{and} smaller arithmetic distance. Only the hybrid policy is competitive
at high $\lambda_A$ weights.

\subsection{Multi-objective score}

We define a composite score:
\[
  \text{score} = \overline{k} + \lambda_H \cdot \overline{\dH} + \lambda_A \cdot \log_2(1 + \overline{|p-x_0|})
\]
where $\overline{\cdot}$ denotes the average over all starting points.

\begin{itemize}
  \item For $\lambda_H = 0$, $\lambda_A = 1$: SOI wins.
  \item For $\lambda_H = 1$, $\lambda_A = 0$: LBF wins.
  \item For $\lambda_H = 1$, $\lambda_A = 0.25$ (default): LBF or Hybrid wins.
\end{itemize}

The optimal strategy is weight-dependent. This confirms the central claim:
the two geometries represent genuinely different objectives.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% ---------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Prime gaps.}
The distribution of gaps between consecutive primes is well-studied
\citep{cramér1936, granville1995, maynard2015}. Our work is complementary:
we study not gaps but Hamming distances, which encode a different notion of proximity.

\paragraph{The binary hypercube.}
The hypercube is a classical object in combinatorics and coding theory
\citep{macwilliams1977, knuth2011}. Boolean functions on the hypercube have
been extensively analyzed \citep{odonnell2014}. The distribution of specific
number-theoretic sets (primes, smooth numbers) as subsets of the hypercube
appears to be less studied.

\paragraph{Primality testing.}
Miller--Rabin \citep{miller1976, rabin1980} and AKS \citep{agrawal2004} are
the standard primality tests. We use sympy's implementation, which is
deterministic for $n < 3.3 \times 10^{24}$.

\paragraph{Bit-flip perturbation in cryptography.}
Bit-flip attacks \citep{kim2014} and fault injection \citep{boneh1997} motivate
the study of which bit flips preserve or destroy primality. Our work provides
a statistical characterization of this phenomenon.

\paragraph{Learned heuristics for number-theoretic search.}
Machine-learning approaches to mathematical discovery have been explored in
\citep{davies2021, romera2023}. Our learned bit-order policy is a simpler
precursor: a count-based heuristic rather than a neural model.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ---------------------------------------------------------------

We have studied prime numbers as vertices in the binary hypercube and compared
Hamming-space prime search with standard arithmetic prime search.

The key findings are:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Hamming density.} Primes are dense in Hamming space.
    Almost every integer is within 2 bit flips of a prime, a direct consequence
    of the rapid growth of Hamming balls.

  \item \textbf{Parity dominates.} In the full hypercube, bit~0 is by far the
    most useful flip direction because it controls parity. After controlling for
    parity (odd subcube), the directional signal is weaker.

  \item \textbf{SOI wins on average; LBF wins on many instances.}
    Standard odd-increment search achieves the lowest average candidate checks.
    Low-bit-first odd-subcube search wins or ties on 58--60\% of individual inputs.

  \item \textbf{Two geometries.} The Pareto frontier for (checks, Hamming distance)
    contains both strategies. They are not competitors; they optimize different
    notions of proximity.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Open questions}

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item \textbf{Larger $m$.} Do the win rates and Pareto structure hold at
    $m \in \{40, 48, 64\}$? The flip-set cache makes this tractable.

  \item \textbf{Hamming-1 prime pairs.} How many pairs of primes $p, q$
    satisfy $\dH(p, q) = 1$ (i.e., differ by a power of 2)?
    What is their distribution?

  \item \textbf{Hypercube sieve.} Can the modular structure of the hypercube
    be used to prescreen candidates, analogous to a sieve in arithmetic space?

  \item \textbf{Modular-residue-aware scoring.} Can bits be scored by their
    direct effect on residues modulo small primes, rather than learned from data?

  \item \textbf{Confidence intervals.} Multiple seeds and bootstrap confidence
    intervals on the win rates and Pareto positions.

  \item \textbf{Neural policies.} Can a neural network learn a better bit-flip
    ordering than the count-based heuristic?
\end{enumerate}

\section{Flip-set construction}
\label{app:flipsets}

For $m$ bits and maximum radius $r_{\max}$, the full list of flip sets in the
odd subcube is:
\[
  \mathcal{F}(m, r_{\max}) = \bigcup_{r=0}^{r_{\max}} \binom{\{1,\ldots,m-1\}}{r}
\]
where $\binom{S}{r}$ denotes all $r$-element subsets of $S$.

The total number of flip sets is $B(m-1, r_{\max})$:
\begin{center}

\section{Primality testing}
\label{app:primality}

We use \texttt{sympy.isprime}, which applies:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Trial division up to a small bound.
  \item Deterministic Miller--Rabin with proven witness sets for $n < 3.3 \times 10^{24}$
    \citep{pomerance1980}.
  \item Probabilistic Miller--Rabin for larger $n$.
\end{itemize}
Results are cached via \texttt{functools.lru\_cache} with capacity $2 \times 10^6$.
For 64-bit numbers, 1000 primality tests take approximately 3.5ms, giving an
estimated total cost of 3.8s for 1.1 million tests (a 50k-sample run at $m=64$).
