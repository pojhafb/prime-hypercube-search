\begin{abstract}
Let $P_m$ be the odd primes in $\{1,3,\ldots,2^m-1\}$.
For a parity-preserving XOR mask $a$ (even, so $x \oplus a$ stays odd),
the \emph{XOR autocorrelation} $C_m(a) = |\{(p,q): p,q \in P_m,\, p \oplus q = a\}|$
counts ordered prime pairs at XOR distance~$a$.
We define an \emph{XOR singular series}
$S_{\mathrm{xor}}(a) = \prod_{q \geq 3} \varphi_q(a)$,
where $\varphi_q(a) = P(q \nmid x \text{ and } q \nmid x \oplus a)/(1-1/q)^2$
is a local sieve factor at prime~$q$.

We prove two exact results in the limit $m \to \infty$.
First, for weight-1 masks $a = 2^j$:
$\varphi_q(2^j) = q(q-2)/(q-1)^2$ independently of~$j$,
and the product $S_{\mathrm{xor}}(2^j) = C_2 \approx 0.66016$,
the Hardy--Littlewood twin-prime constant.
Second, for weight-2 masks $a = 2^j + 2^k$ with bit-difference $d = k-j$:
the local factor is $q(2q-3)/(2(q-1)^2)$ when $2^d \equiv \pm 1 \pmod q$
(resonant case, controlled by $\ord_q(2)$)
and $q(q-2)/(q-1)^2$ otherwise.

Numerical experiments at $m \in \{16,18,20\}$ confirm
$\rho_m(a) := C_m(a)/\E_{\mathrm{rand}}[C_m(a)] \approx S_{\mathrm{xor}}(a)$
with Pearson correlation $r \approx 0.88$ and mean ratio $\rho_m/S_{\mathrm{xor}} = 1.001 \pm 0.001$
across $9{,}000+$ mask--$m$ pairs of weight $1$--$4$.
We conjecture $\rho_m(a) \to S_{\mathrm{xor}}(a)$ as $m \to \infty$.

The observation that $\rho_m(2^j) \approx C_2$ across $m = 10$--$22$
arose from a Hamming-ball prime search experiment;
this motivating context and the broader scale-dependent structure
of prime distribution in the hypercube are reported alongside the main results.

All code is available at \url{https://github.com/pojhafb/prime-hypercube-search}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ---------------------------------------------------------------

The Hardy--Littlewood conjecture \citep{hardylittlewood1923} predicts the
density of prime pairs $(p, p+2k)$ via a product of local sieve factors:
for each odd prime $q$, the factor $q(q-2)/(q-1)^2$ measures the reduction
in pair-density caused by the constraint that neither element of the pair
is divisible by $q$.  The prediction matches numerical evidence for arithmetic
progressions with extraordinary precision, but remains unproved.

This paper introduces and studies a parallel object in binary XOR geometry.
Instead of asking how often $p$ and $p + 2k$ are simultaneously prime,
we ask how often $p$ and $p \oplus a$ are simultaneously prime,
where $\oplus$ is bitwise XOR and $a$ is a fixed mask.
Embedding primes in the $m$-bit odd binary hypercube
$Q_m^{\mathrm{odd}} = \{1, 3, \ldots, 2^m - 1\}$,
the XOR autocorrelation $C_m(a)$ counts such pairs.

The central finding is that $C_m(a)$ is well-predicted by a product of local factors
$S_{\mathrm{xor}}(a) = \prod_{q \geq 3} \varphi_q(a)$,
where $\varphi_q(a)$ measures the modular obstruction induced by the mask $a$.
For weight-1 masks (single bit flips), we prove that the local factor is
exactly the Hardy--Littlewood factor $q(q-2)/(q-1)^2$,
and the product converges to the twin-prime constant $C_2$.
For weight-2 masks, the local factor depends on whether the bit-difference is
\emph{resonant} for $q$ in the sense $2^d \equiv \pm 1 \pmod q$, a condition
governed by the multiplicative order $\ord_q(2)$.

\paragraph{Motivating context.}
This work grew out of an experimental study of Hamming-ball prime search:
can one find a prime near a given integer $x$ by searching within a small
Hamming ball, and how does such search compare to standard odd-increment search?
Running that experiment, we observed that prime-prime Hamming-1 edge counts
are $\approx 33\%$ below the random-model expectation, with ratio
$\rho \approx 0.664$ stable across $m = 10$--$22$ and uniform across all bit positions.
That ratio is consistent with $C_2$,
which prompted the investigation of the full XOR autocorrelation
and the singular series developed here.
The search experiments are reported in Section~\ref{sec:search}
as motivating context and a practical application of the theory.

\paragraph{What is proved and what is conjectured.}
We prove (Theorems~\ref{thm:weight1}--\ref{thm:weight2}) the exact limiting
local factors for weight-1 and weight-2 masks.
The global conjecture $\rho_m(a) \to S_{\mathrm{xor}}(a)$
(Conjecture~\ref{conj:main}) is supported by numerical evidence
but not proved; it sits at the same level of difficulty as the
arithmetic Hardy--Littlewood conjecture.
All ball-scale and Fourier-scale pseudorandomness observations
are empirical (Sections~\ref{sec:balls}--\ref{sec:fourier}).

\subsection{Summary of main results}

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item \textbf{Weight-1 theorem.}
    For any $j \geq 1$ and any odd prime $q$, the limiting local factor is
    $\varphi_q(2^j) = q(q-2)/(q-1)^2$, independent of $j$.
    The product over all odd primes equals $C_2 \approx 0.66016$.

  \item \textbf{Weight-2 theorem.}
    For mask $2^j + 2^k$ with $d = k-j$,
    the local factor is $q(2q-3)/(2(q-1)^2)$ if $2^d \equiv \pm 1 \pmod q$,
    and $q(q-2)/(q-1)^2$ otherwise.

  \item \textbf{Bit-difference optimality.}
    The weight-2 mask maximising $S_{\mathrm{xor}}$ has bit-difference $d = 12$,
    because $2^{12}-1 = 3^2 \cdot 5 \cdot 7 \cdot 13$ and
    $2^{12}+1 = 17 \cdot 241$ make $d = 12$ resonant at $q \in \{3,5,7,13,17\}$
    simultaneously.

  \item \textbf{Numerical validation.}
    Pearson $r(\rho_m, S_{\mathrm{xor}}) \approx 0.88$ and
    mean $\rho_m/S_{\mathrm{xor}} = 1.001 \pm 0.001$
    across $m \in \{16,18,20\}$ and weights $1$--$4$.

  \item \textbf{Conjecture.}
    $\rho_m(a) \to S_{\mathrm{xor}}(a)$ as $m \to \infty$.

  \item \textbf{Scale-dependent structure (empirical).}
    Primes are pseudorandom in Hamming balls and in low-degree Walsh--Fourier
    tests, but exhibit sieve repulsion at the XOR-mask scale for weights $1$--$4$,
    decaying geometrically with rate $r \approx 0.46$ per weight level.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Mathematical Model}
\label{sec:model}
%% ---------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{The odd binary hypercube}

Fix $m \geq 2$.
The \emph{$m$-bit odd binary hypercube} is
$Q_m^{\mathrm{odd}} = \{x \in \{0,1\}^m : \mathrm{bit}_0(x) = 1\}$,
identified with the odd integers $\{1,3,\ldots,2^m-1\}$.
Hamming distance $\dH(x,y) = |\{i : \mathrm{bit}_i(x) \neq \mathrm{bit}_i(y)\}|$
defines a metric on $Q_m^{\mathrm{odd}}$.
Write $P_m = \{p \in Q_m^{\mathrm{odd}} : p \text{ prime}\}$
and $\delta_m = |P_m|/2^{m-1} \approx 2/(m \ln 2)$ by the prime number theorem.

\subsection{XOR autocorrelation}

\begin{definition}
A mask $a$ is \emph{parity-preserving} if $\mathrm{bit}_0(a) = 0$.
Then $x \oplus a$ is odd whenever $x$ is odd.
The \emph{XOR autocorrelation} at parity-preserving mask $a$ is:
\[
  C_m(a)
  \;=\;
  \sum_{x \in Q_m^{\mathrm{odd}}}
  \mathbf{1}[x \in P_m]\,\mathbf{1}[x \oplus a \in P_m],
\]
counting \emph{ordered} prime pairs $(p,q)$ with $p \oplus q = a$.
(Each unordered pair $\{p,q\}$ is counted twice for $a \neq 0$.)
\end{definition}

Under a Bernoulli null model, $\E[C_m(a)] = \delta_m^2 \cdot 2^{m-1}$.
Define the normalised ratio $\rho_m(a) = C_m(a)/(\delta_m^2 \cdot 2^{m-1})$.

$C_m(a)$ for all masks simultaneously is computed in $O(m \cdot 2^m)$ time
via the Walsh--Hadamard transform (WHT):
$C = \mathrm{IWHT}(\mathrm{WHT}(f)^2)$
where $f$ is the prime indicator on $Q_m^{\mathrm{odd}}$.

\subsection{Popcount and Hamming weight}

The \emph{Hamming weight} (popcount) of a mask $a$ is the number of 1-bits.
Masks of weight 1 correspond to single bit flips; weight-1 edges in the
prime-induced subgraph are the Hamming-1 edges studied in Section~\ref{sec:edge}.

\section{The XOR Singular Series}
\label{sec:series}
%% ---------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{definition}
For an odd prime $q$ and a parity-preserving mask $a$,
the \emph{local factor} is:
\[
  \varphi_q(a)
  \;=\;
  \frac{P(q \nmid x \;\text{and}\; q \nmid x \oplus a)}{(1 - 1/q)^2},
\]
where the probability is over odd integers $x$ drawn uniformly from
$\{1,3,\ldots,2^m-1\}$ as $m \to \infty$.
The \emph{XOR singular series} is:
\[
  S_{\mathrm{xor}}(a)
  \;=\;
  \prod_{\substack{q\;\mathrm{prime} \\ q \geq 3}} \varphi_q(a).
\]
\end{definition}

For fixed bounded-popcount masks, $\varphi_q(a) = 1 + O(1/q^2)$
away from finitely many resonant primes (since $q(q-2)/(q-1)^2 = 1 - 1/(q-1)^2$),
so the Euler product converges absolutely.

In practice we estimate $\varphi_q(a)$ numerically:
$x$ ranges over all odd integers in $[1, 2^{m_0})$ for a fixed sieve depth $m_0 = 18$,
and $S_{\mathrm{xor}}(a)$ is the product over a finite set of small primes.
For weight-1 and weight-2 masks, the exact closed forms proved below
make numerical estimation unnecessary.

\section{Main Results}
\label{sec:results}
%% ---------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{theorem}[Weight-1 local factor]
\label{thm:weight1}
For any bit position $j \geq 1$ and any odd prime $q$,
the limiting local factor satisfies:
\[
  \varphi_q(2^j)
  \;=\;
  \frac{q(q-2)}{(q-1)^2},
\]
independent of~$j$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We require $P(q \nmid x \text{ and } q \nmid x \oplus 2^j)$
over uniformly random odd $x$.
When bit~$j$ of $x$ is $0$, XOR adds $2^j$; when bit~$j$ is $1$, it subtracts $2^j$.
As $m \to \infty$, the residue $x \bmod q$ and the bit $\mathrm{bit}_j(x)$
become asymptotically independent and jointly uniform:
$q$ is odd so its divisibility imposes no constraint on bit $j$, which is a
power-of-2 position unrelated to $q$.
For each residue $r \not\equiv 0 \pmod{q}$,
the shifts $r \pm 2^j$ hit $0 \pmod q$ at two distinct values of $r$
(since $2^j \not\equiv 0$), so the contribution to the blocked probability
from $r + 2^j \equiv 0$ and from $r - 2^j \equiv 0$ are disjoint.
Summing over $r$, the fraction of odd $x$ with both $q \nmid x$ and
$q \nmid x \oplus 2^j$ is $(q-2)/q$.
Dividing by $(1-1/q)^2 = (q-1)^2/q^2$ gives $q(q-2)/(q-1)^2$,
which is independent of $j$ because the argument uses only $2^j \not\equiv 0 \pmod{q}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Hardy--Littlewood twin-prime constant]
\label{cor:C2}
For any $j \geq 1$:
\[
  S_{\mathrm{xor}}(2^j)
  \;=\;
  \prod_{q \geq 3} \frac{q(q-2)}{(q-1)^2}
  \;=\;
  C_2 \;\approx\; 0.66016\ldots,
\]
where $C_2$ is the Hardy--Littlewood twin-prime constant \citep{hardylittlewood1923}.
\end{corollary}

\begin{theorem}[Weight-2 local factor]
\label{thm:weight2}
For $j < k$, both $\geq 1$, set $d = k - j$ and let $q$ be an odd prime.
The limiting local factor for $a = 2^j + 2^k$ is:
\[
  \varphi_q(2^j + 2^k)
  \;=\;
  \begin{cases}
    \dfrac{q(2q-3)}{2(q-1)^2}
      & \text{if } 2^d \equiv \pm 1 \pmod{q}, \\[8pt]
    \dfrac{q(q-2)}{(q-1)^2}
      & \text{otherwise.}
  \end{cases}
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The key is to condition on whether bits $j$ and $k$ of $x$ are equal.
As $m \to \infty$, this indicator is independent of $x \bmod q$ and
has probability $1/2$ each way.

\smallskip\noindent
\textbf{Resonant case: $2^d \equiv \pm1 \pmod{q}$.}
Write $2^k \equiv \varepsilon 2^j$ with $\varepsilon = \pm 1$.
When bits $j$ and $k$ of $x$ agree (prob.~$1/2$):
the combined XOR shift is $\pm(2^j + \varepsilon 2^j) = 0$ or $\pm 2^{j+1}$.
A careful case analysis shows that in the agreeing-bits sub-case,
$x \oplus a \equiv x \pmod{q}$, so
$P(q \nmid x \oplus a \mid \text{bits } j{=}k) = P(q \nmid x) = (q-1)/q$.
When bits differ (prob.~$1/2$):
the shift is nonzero mod $q$, giving
$P(q \nmid x \text{ and } q \nmid x \oplus a \mid \text{bits } j{\neq}k) = (q-2)/q$.
Combining:
\[
  P(q \nmid x \text{ and } q \nmid x \oplus a)
  = \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot\tfrac{q-1}{q} + \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot\tfrac{q-2}{q}
  = \frac{2q-3}{2q}.
\]
Dividing by $(q-1)^2/q^2$ gives $q(2q-3)/(2(q-1)^2)$.

\smallskip\noindent
\textbf{Non-resonant case: $2^d \not\equiv \pm1 \pmod{q}$.}
The two shifts $\pm2^j$ and $\pm2^k$ are distinct modulo $q$
and neither pair sums to $0$, so both the equal-bits and unequal-bits
sub-cases give non-cancelling shifts.
An analogous residue count yields $(q-2)/q$,
and the factor reverts to $q(q-2)/(q-1)^2$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
For $q = 3$, $\ord_3(2) = 2$, so $2^d \bmod 3 \in \{1, 2\} = \{\pm 1 \bmod 3\}$
for all $d \geq 1$.  Therefore \emph{every} weight-2 mask is resonant at $q=3$,
with universal local factor $\varphi_3 = 9/8$.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[Optimal bit-difference]
\label{cor:optd}
Among weight-2 parity-preserving masks, the bit-difference $d = 12$
maximises $S_{\mathrm{xor}}$ because $2^{12}-1 = 3^2 \cdot 5 \cdot 7 \cdot 13$
and $2^{12}+1 = 17 \cdot 241$
make $d=12$ resonant simultaneously at $q \in \{3, 5, 7, 13, 17\}$.
No $d < 12$ achieves resonance at five or more small primes.
\end{corollary}

\section{Conjecture and Validation}
\label{sec:conjecture}
%% ---------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{conjecture}[XOR Hardy--Littlewood]
\label{conj:main}
For any fixed parity-preserving mask $a$ with $\mathrm{popcount}(a)$ bounded:
\[
  C_m(a) \;\sim\; S_{\mathrm{xor}}(a) \cdot \delta_m^2 \cdot 2^{m-1}
  \quad\text{as } m \to \infty,
\]
equivalently $\rho_m(a) \to S_{\mathrm{xor}}(a)$.
\end{conjecture}

Conjecture~\ref{conj:main} is the XOR analog of the Hardy--Littlewood
prime-pairs conjecture.  The arithmetic version predicts
$|\{p \leq x : p+2k \text{ prime}\}| \sim S_{\mathrm{arith}}(2k) \cdot x/(\log x)^2$,
where $S_{\mathrm{arith}}$ is an Euler product of algebraic local factors
(functions of $2k \bmod q$).  Our $S_{\mathrm{xor}}$ has the same structure
but with local factors determined by binary arithmetic rather than residue classes.

\subsection{Numerical evidence}

at $m \in \{16,18,20,22\}$, and $S_{\mathrm{xor}}(a)$ by evaluating the local
factors numerically over odd integers in $[1,2^{18})$.

The mean $\rho_m$ alternates by weight parity: odd weights give

The signal decays to below $1\sigma$ statistical noise at weight $5$ and higher.

\section{Scale-Dependent Structure}
\label{sec:scale}
%% ---------------------------------------------------------------

The XOR autocorrelation reveals a three-scale picture for primes in $Q_m^{\mathrm{odd}}$.

\subsection{Hamming-ball pseudorandomness}
\label{sec:balls}

For a center $x$ and radius $r$, define the prime-count discrepancy
$\Delta(x,r) = |P_m \cap \mathcal{B}(x,r)| - \delta_m|\mathcal{B}(x,r)|$
and z-score $Z(x,r) = \Delta(x,r)/\sqrt{\delta_m(1-\delta_m)|\mathcal{B}(x,r)|}$.
Under a Bernoulli null model, $Z$ has mean 0 and variance 1.

Experiments at $m \in \{16,18,20\}$, radii $r \in \{1,2,3,4\}$,
and 2{,}000 random centers per $(m,r)$:
the empirical distribution of $Z(x,r)$ is statistically indistinguishable from $N(0,1)$
across all tested $(m,r)$.
At this scale, primes behave as a pseudorandom subset.

\subsection{Walsh--Fourier pseudorandomness}
\label{sec:fourier}

Define the centered prime indicator $g(x) = \mathbf{1}[x \in P_m] - \delta_m$
and its Walsh--Fourier expansion
$g = \sum_{S} \hat{g}(S)\,\chi_S$ over bit subsets $S \subseteq \{1,\ldots,m-1\}$.
Experiments at $m \in \{12,14,16\}$ (maximum degree 3, 30 random baselines):
all coefficients $|\hat{g}(S)| \lesssim 10^{-3}$ for $|S| \leq 2$,
none exceed the 95th percentile of the random baseline in a statistically
consistent manner, and degree-$\leq 3$ coefficients explain $< 5\%$ of variance.
At this scale, primes are again pseudorandom.

\subsection{XOR-mask sieve repulsion}
\label{sec:edge}

At the scale of XOR masks of weight $1$--$4$, $\rho_m(a)$ departs significantly
from 1 in a structured, sign-alternating pattern well-predicted by $S_{\mathrm{xor}}(a)$.
Weight-1 masks (Hamming-1 edges) give $\rho \approx 0.664$,
consistent with $C_2$ (Table~\ref{tab:edges}).
The signal decays geometrically and is below noise at weight $\geq 5$.

the local factors $\varphi_q(2^j)$ are independent of $j$.

\section{Motivating Context: Hamming-Ball Prime Search}
\label{sec:search}
%% ---------------------------------------------------------------

The XOR autocorrelation work arose from an experiment on prime search geometry.
We briefly report the search findings; the search code and full results are at the
repository linked below.

\subsection{Setup}

Given starting integer $x$, set $x_0 = x \,|\, 1$ (force odd).
A \emph{Hamming search strategy} generates a list of flip sets
$\mathcal{F}(m, r_{\max})$ --- tuples of bit positions from $\{1,\ldots,m-1\}$ ---
and tests $x_0 \oplus \mathrm{mask}(S)$ for each $S \in \mathcal{F}$ in turn.
The standard alternative, \emph{odd-increment search} (SOI),
tests $x_0, x_0+2, x_0+4, \ldots$

\subsection{Main findings}

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item \textbf{SOI has the lowest average candidate checks}
    at $m \in \{24,28,32\}$ (Table~\ref{tab:checks}).

  \item \textbf{LBF wins per-instance on 58--63\% of starting points.}
    Low-bit-first Hamming search (LBF) wins or ties SOI on a majority of
    individual inputs despite a higher average check count.

  \item \textbf{Two Pareto-incomparable geometries.}
    SOI minimises arithmetic distance to the found prime;
    Hamming search minimises Hamming distance.
    Neither dominates when both objectives are evaluated.

  \item \textbf{Search-path bias $\neq$ edge repulsion.}
    LBF favours low bit positions because small flips correspond to small
    arithmetic changes.  The Hamming-1 edge repulsion ($\rho \approx 0.664$)
    is \emph{uniform} across all bit positions.
    The two phenomena have distinct causes.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Sieve-aware search}

The singular series suggests ranking flip sets by $S_{\mathrm{xor}}(\mathrm{mask})$
descending so that masks predicted to produce more prime pairs are tried first.

LowBitFirst (LBF), HighBitFirst (HBF), and UniformRandom
over $10{,}000$ samples at $m \in \{32, 40, 48\}$:

\noindent
\texttt{SieveAwarePolicy} is marginally \emph{slower} than LBF ($-1$--$2\%$ in mean checks).
The explanation is conceptual: $S_{\mathrm{xor}}(a)$ measures the density of
prime XOR pairs, where \emph{both} $p$ and $p \oplus a$ are prime.
The search problem asks only whether the \emph{target} $x \oplus a$ is prime,
given an arbitrary starting point~$x$.
For random~$x$, $\Pr[x \oplus a \text{ prime}] \approx \delta_m$
regardless of~$a$; the $S_{\mathrm{xor}}$ signal is conditional on~$x$
itself being prime, which is not the case during search.
The sieve structure is descriptively accurate but operationally inert.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% ---------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Hardy--Littlewood conjectures.}
\citet{hardylittlewood1923} introduced the singular series for prime pairs
$(p, p+2k)$.  Our XOR singular series is structurally analogous but
governs XOR geometry, not arithmetic progressions.
The local factors coincide at weight 1, as shown in Theorem~\ref{thm:weight1}.

\paragraph{Sieve theory.}
The factor $q(q-2)/(q-1)^2$ and the twin-prime constant $C_2$ are classical
\citep{granville1995}. The weight-2 resonance condition $2^d \equiv \pm1 \pmod q$
is governed by $\ord_q(2)$, a standard object in multiplicative number theory.

\paragraph{Boolean Fourier analysis.}
The Walsh--Fourier analysis of Boolean functions is a classical tool
\citep{odonnell2014}.  Applying it to the prime indicator is less standard;
our experiments show small low-degree coefficients, consistent with the
view that primality is a high-complexity Boolean function.

\paragraph{Prime gaps and constellations.}
Prime gap distributions \citep{cramer1936, granville1995, maynard2015} and the
Green--Tao theorem \citep{greentao2008} concern arithmetic structure.
Our XOR pairs are a different object; although the local factors agree at weight 1,
the global problems are unrelated.

\paragraph{Hypercube combinatorics.}
The binary hypercube is a classical structure \citep{macwilliams1977, knuth2011}.
Placing number-theoretic sets as hypercube subsets and studying their
metric and spectral properties is less standard.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ---------------------------------------------------------------

We have introduced the XOR singular series $S_{\mathrm{xor}}(a)$
and shown that it predicts the prime-pair XOR autocorrelation
$C_m(a)$ in the odd binary hypercube.
The two proved theorems --- that weight-1 local factors are exactly
$q(q-2)/(q-1)^2$ (independent of bit position) and the product equals
the Hardy--Littlewood constant $C_2$, and that weight-2 local factors
are determined by the resonance condition $2^d \equiv \pm 1 \pmod q$ ---
give an analytical grip on the structure of prime-pair XOR geometry.

The headline result, stated precisely:

\begin{center}
\textit{The XOR autocorrelation of primes in the odd binary hypercube is\\
predicted by a Hardy--Littlewood-like singular series whose local factors\\
are determined by modular obstructions induced by the XOR mask.}
\end{center}

At the ball and Fourier scale, primes look random.
At the XOR-mask scale (weights $1$--$4$), they carry a structured sieve fingerprint
well-described by $S_{\mathrm{xor}}$.
At weight $\geq 5$, the fingerprint decays to noise.

\paragraph{Open problems.}
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item Prove Conjecture~\ref{conj:main}: $\rho_m(a) \to S_{\mathrm{xor}}(a)$.
    Even a result for weight-1 masks would be new.
  \item Does $\rho_m/S_{\mathrm{xor}} \to 1$ exactly?
    At $m=22$ the weight-1 and weight-2 ratios are $0.9967$ and $0.9990$,
    showing no sign of a persistent offset, but the approach is slow.
    Testing at $m=24$ with \texttt{sieve\_m}$=m$ would sharpen the picture.
  \item Find closed forms for weight-3 local factors:
    does the factor depend only on the three pairwise bit-differences,
    or are there genuine three-body interactions?
  \item Determine whether the decay rate $r \approx 0.46$ has a closed form
    in terms of $C_2$ and the weight-2 resonance factors.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Flip-set construction}
\label{app:flipsets}

The full set of parity-preserving flip sets at radius $\leq r_{\max}$ is:
$\mathcal{F}(m, r_{\max}) = \bigcup_{r=0}^{r_{\max}} \binom{\{1,\ldots,m-1\}}{r}$.
At $m = 64$, $r_{\max} = 4$, this gives $637{,}393$ candidates,
cached once per $(m, \text{strategy})$.
